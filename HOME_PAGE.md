# Home Page

## Standard Homepage Sections

Commonly recognized blocks beyond the hero and testimonials include:

- **Value Proposition (Hero)**
- **Social Proof Logo Bar:** A horizontal row of recognizable company logos placed immediately below the hero to build instant credibility.
- **The Problem/Stakes Section:** Clearly articulates the specific pain points or challenges your target audience faces before introducing your product as the solution.
- **The Solution/How It Works:** A bridge section that introduces your product and explains the high-level mechanism of how it solves the aforementioned problem.
- **Feature/Benefit Blocks:** These are often "alternating" layouts (image on left, text on right, then flipped) that focus on specific product capabilities.
- **Integration Section:** A dedicated block, often with a grid of logos, showing which other software tools your product connects with (e.g., Slack, Salesforce).
- **Pricing Teaser:** A simplified version of your pricing table that helps qualify leads before they click through to a full pricing page.
- **FAQ Section:** A list of frequently asked questions to proactively address common objections or technical concerns.
- **Closing CTA**

### Problem-Solution-Proof Flow

| Practice            | Recommendation                                               |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Section Count**   | Aim for **6 to 9 sections** total. Too many can cause "scroll fatigue," while too few may leave objections unanswered. |
| **Sequencing**      | Start with **Value Proposition** (Hero), follow with **Trust** (Logos), then **The Problem**, then **Features/Benefits**, and end with a **Closing CTA**. |
| **CTA Frequency**   | Include a primary CTA (e.g., "Start Free Trial") in the **Header, Hero, middle of the page, and at the very bottom**. |
| **Content Balance** | Focus on **benefits over features**. Instead of just listing a tool (feature), explain the positive outcome for the user (benefit). |

### Problem Section Copywriting

The goal here is to make the visitor feel understood by "agitating" their current pain points before offering your product as the cure. A common framework used is **PAS** (Problem, Agitate, Solve).

- **The "Status Quo" Approach:** Describe the messy reality of their current workflow.
  - *Example:* "Stop chasing developers for simple copy changes."
- **The "Cost of Inaction" Approach:** Highlight what is being lost (time, money, or sanity) by not solving the issue.
  - *Example:* "Cold outreach won't help you quickly acquire customers if your email list is unreliable."
- **The "Finally" Headline:** Position your product as the long-awaited fix for a known industry-wide issue.
  - *Example:* "We finally fixed your email + calendar."

### Pricing Table Design Best Practices

A well-designed pricing table reduces "choice paralysis" and highlights the most profitable or popular option.

- **The "Recommended" Badge:** Visually distinguish one plan (usually the middle one) as the "Most Popular" or "Best Value" to nudge users toward it.
- **Feature Comparison:** Use checkmarks for included features and simple "—" or "X" icons for excluded ones to make differences scannable.
- **Tiered Personas:** Name your tiers after the specific users they serve, such as "Starter," "Business," and "Enterprise".
- **The Annual Toggle:** Include a switch to show "Monthly vs. Yearly" pricing. Standard practice is to offer a 15–25% discount for annual billing to improve cash flow.
- **Proximity of FAQ:** Place 3–5 FAQs immediately below the pricing table to handle common billing objections right at the point of decision.
